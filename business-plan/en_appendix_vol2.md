## Chapter VII. East Asian Traditional Medicine

Of all regions of the habitable globe, none may boast so dense a concentration of indigenous medical systems as the vast and ancient land of Eastern Asia. Within this territory, no fewer than eight distinct traditions — the Medicine of China, the Medicine of Tibet, the Medicine of Mongolia, the Medicine of the Uyghurs, the Medicine of the Miao, the Medicine of Korea, the Japanese Kampō, and the Medicine of Vietnam — have arisen, flourished, and endured, each in its own manner bearing witness to that fundamental principle which the present work denominates the holonic embryo: that the part doth faithfully reflect the whole.

Let us commence our survey with Tibet, whose medical inheritance finds its supreme expression in the rGyud-bZhi, or *Four Tantras*, compiled in the eighth century of the Christian era by the physician Gyu-thog Yon-tan mGon-po. This monumental corpus, comprising some two hundred thousand characters, is divided into four books: the Root Tantra, of six chapters, setting forth the general principles of medicine; the Explanatory Tantra, in thirty-one chapters, wherein diseases are classified; the Instructional Tantra, of ninety-two chapters, devoted to clinical practice; and the Subsequent Tantra, of twenty-seven chapters, which treateth of diagnosis and pharmacy. The theoretical edifice of Tibetan medicine resteth upon the doctrine of Three Humours — rLung, or Wind, presiding over respiration and motion; mKhris-pa, or Bile, governing heat, digestion, and metabolism; and Bad-kan, or Phlegm, controlling the fluids, mucus, and structural integrity of the body. Health is that happy state wherein the three humours are in equilibrium; disease, their lamentable discord. To this framework is joined a system of seven bodily constituents — chyme, blood, flesh, fat, bone, marrow, and semen — and three excretions — faeces, urine, and sweat. The diagnostic art of the Tibetan physician is distinguished from that of his neighbours by his extraordinary reliance upon both pulse examination, wherein no fewer than thirty distinct qualities are recognised, and upon uroscopy, wherein nine separate observations — colour, vapour, odour, foam, sediment, turbidity, temperature, surface oil, and the changes wrought by time — are systematically recorded.

The Medicine of Mongolia, which received its definitive form in the eighteenth century through the labours of Ishbaljir, is a synthesis of Tibetan, Chinese, and Central Asian traditions. Its distinctive contribution is the doctrine of Six Root Affections: Hii (Wind), relating to the nervous system; Shira (Bile), pertaining to heat; Badgan (Phlegm), appertaining to cold; Blood-Heat; Yellow-Water, or Humid; and Worm, or Parasite. The therapeutic arsenal of the Mongolian physician is remarkably varied, embracing herbal remedies, a unique tradition of bone-setting, bloodletting, and the quintuple regimen of oil therapy, moxibustion, fomentation,温泉 bathing, and puncture. We may observe that the Six Affections exceed the Three Humours of Tibet by three — Blood-Heat, Yellow-Water, and Worm — a difference that plainly reflecteth the harsh environment of the Mongolian plateau, with its extreme cold and endemic parasitism.

The Medicine of the Uyghurs, practiced in the oases of the Tarim Basin, representeth a marriage of the Greek Humoral Theory with Chinese, Arabic, and indigenous traditions. Its foundation is the doctrine of Four Humours: Sapra (Yellow Bile), hot and dry; Kan (Blood), hot and moist; Balgham (Phlegm), cold and moist; and Savda (Black Bile), cold and dry. To these correspond eight Temperaments, or Mizaj. Among its therapeutic methods, none is more singular than the Sand Therapy of Turfan, wherein patients are buried in desert sands heated by the summer sun to temperatures between sixty and eighty degrees centigrade, a practice whose efficacy for rheumatic and dermatological disorders hath been confirmed by modern investigation.

The Medicine of the Miao, inhabiting the mountainous regions of southwestern China, is organised upon the principle of Two Cardinal Guides — Cold and Heat — and Five Passages: Cold, Heat, Swift, Slow, and Transformative. Their pharmacopoeia, exceeding five hundred simples, is administered through decoction, external application, and the celebrated herbal bath.

We turn now to Korea, where the physician Yi Che-ma, in the year 1894 of the Christian era, published his *Dongui Suse Bowon* and thereby founded the system of Sasang, or Four-Constitutional Medicine. This bold innovation divideth all humankind into four constitutional types: the Tai-Yang, or Great Yang, prone to excitatory disorders; the Shao-Yang, or Lesser Yang, inclined to febrile conditions; the Tai-Yin, or Great Yin, susceptible to damp afflictions; and the Shao-Yin, or Lesser Yin, disposed to cold diseases. The fundamental insight — that constitutional type determineth disease predisposition — marketh Yi Che-ma as a precursor of precision medicine, and the holonic character of his system is evident: the constitution, a local expression of the individual, becometh the key to the whole.

The Japanese Kampō, as reformed by Yoshimasu Tōdō in the eighteenth century, pursued an opposite path to that of Korea, abandoning the elaborate diagnostic apparatus of Chinese medicine in favour of a rigorous empiricism. Its cardinal principle, *Hōshō Sōtai*, asserteth a direct correspondence between formula and syndrome: each prescription answereth to a specific pattern of disease, and the physician need not speculate upon hidden causes. One hundred and forty-eight classical formulae are recognised by the Japanese health authorities, and it is a remarkable fact that more than eighty percent of Japanese physicians have at some time prescribed these remedies.

The Vietnamese tradition, known as Thuốc Nam, or Southern Medicine, coexists with Thuốc Bắc, or Northern Medicine (that is, Chinese medicine). Its great systematiser, Hải Thượng Lãn Ông, or Lê Hữu Trác, who flourished in the eighteenth century, compiled the *Nam Dược Thần Hiệu*, or Miraculous Effects of Southern Drugs, drawing upon a pharmacopoeia of more than four thousand tropical simples.

If we survey these eight systems with the eye of a natural philosopher, we discern a unity that transcendeth their manifest diversity. Each, in its own idiom, affirmeth that the part is a faithful epitome of the whole — that the pulse at the wrist revealeth the state of every organ, that the countenance mirroreth the condition of the viscera, that the constitution is a microcosm of the cosmos. The eight systems together constitute the richest clinical repository of holonic medicine upon the face of the earth, and any modern apparatus that aspireth to incorporate traditional medical wisdom — such as the HoloScan 2.0 proposed in these pages — must perforce accommodate their methods, their pharmacopoeiae, and their constitutional typologies.

## Chapter VIII. Tibetan Plateau and Highland Medicine

The highlands of Central Asia, where the air is thin and the sun fierce, where winter endureth for half the year and the earth is locked in frost, have given birth to medical traditions of extraordinary resilience and originality. In the present chapter we shall examine the four principal systems of this region — Tibetan, Mongolian, Uyghur, and Miao — and demonstrate how each, in responding to the exigencies of its particular environment, hath independently arrived at a holonic understanding of health and disease.

The Medicine of Tibet, which we have already introduced in its general outline, merits a more detailed exposition. Its intimate connection with Indian Buddhism is not to be doubted; the doctrine of Three Humours is manifestly derived, in part, from the Indian Tridosha, while the concept of seven bodily constituents and three excretions representeth a synthesis of Indian and Chinese elements. Yet Tibetan medicine is no mere copy; it is a creative fusion, shaped by the unique conditions of the Plateau. The pulse examination, to which we have alluded, exceedeth even the sophistication of Chinese sphygmology, recognising subdivisions of the tense and relaxed qualities unknown to the physicians of the Middle Kingdom. But it is in uroscopy that Tibetan medicine achieveth its most remarkable diagnostic refinement. The nine dimensions of urinary observation — a system more thorough than any other known to medical history — permit the physician to read the state of the entire organism in a single fluid excretion. The colour, from white through yellow and red to black; the vapour, whether abundant or scant, persistent or evanescent; the odour, normal or offensive; the foam, its size, quantity, and duration; the sediment, its presence, absence, and form; the turbidity, clear or cloudy; the temperature, cold, warm, or hot; the surface oil, present or absent; and the changes wrought by time — each of these nine observations yieldeth information about the three humours and their equilibrium. This is holonic diagnosis in its most refined form: the urine, a local product, becometh a complete map of the body's internal state.

The therapeutic methods of Tibet are no less remarkable. The Five-Ingredient Herbal Bath, composed of five high-altitude plants — juniper, rhododendron, tamarisk, ephedra, and wormwood — hath been employed for centuries in the treatment of rheumatic, dermatological, and arthritic disorders. Modern pharmacological research hath confirmed its anti-inflammatory properties, and the Tibetan hospitals of contemporary China, numbering more than two hundred, treat annually a vast multitude of patients.

The Medicine of Mongolia, we have noted, addeth three affections to the Tibetan Three. This expansion is not arbitrary; it reflecteth the medical realities of the Mongolian steppe. The Blood-Heat affection correspondeth, in modern terms, to states of circulatory excitement; Yellow-Water, to conditions of oedema and dampness; and Worm, to the parasitic infections that were endemic in traditional pastoral life. The Mongolian bone-setting tradition, recognised as a national intangible cultural heritage in 2008, is a practice of singular interest. The practitioner, without recourse to surgery, effecteth the natural reduction of fractures, employing a technique that includeth the spraying of alcohol from the mouth upon the affected part, the application of leather splints, and the early commencement of functional exercise. The Five Therapies of Mongolian medicine — oil therapy, moxibustion, fomentation, hot spring bathing, and puncture — all operate upon the same holonic principle: an external, local stimulus produceth an internal, systemic response.

The Medicine of the Uyghurs, practiced in the Tarim Basin, presents us with the spectacle of a system that hath synthesised elements from four continents. The Greek Humoral Theory, transmitted through Arabic medicine, was here blended with Chinese, Persian, and indigenous traditions. The Sand Therapy of Turfan, already mentioned, is a treatment of extraordinary ingenuity. During the summer months, when the desert sands reach temperatures of sixty to eighty degrees centigrade, patients are buried in them for periods of twenty to thirty minutes. The therapeutic mechanism is threefold: the heat itself, the far-infrared radiation emitted by the sand particles, and the mineral absorption through the skin. The local application of heat produceth a systemic response — the whole body is treated through the partial immersion of its surface.

The Medicine of the Miao, who inhabit the mountainous terrain of Guizhou and Yunnan, is founded upon a distinctive doctrine of Toxicity. "No poison, no disease; no poison, no cure," the Miao physicians declare. Disease is conceived as a state of poisoning, herbal remedies as antidotes, and health as the equilibrium between toxin and antitoxin. The Miao herbal bath, administered to newborns for prophylaxis, to parturient women for recovery, and to rheumatic patients for long-term treatment, is a multi-herbal decoction whose composition is adjusted to the individual patient — a practice that recogniseth the unique configuration of each body.

The geographical determinism of these four systems is striking and instructive. The Tibetan physician treateth the diseases of hypoxia and ultraviolet radiation; the Mongolian, the afflictions of drought and cold; the Uyghur, the conditions of extreme aridity and heat; the Miao, the disorders of humidity and mountainous terrain. Each system is, in the truest sense, a holonic expression of its environment: the land, in its particularity, hath impressed itself upon the medicine. Modern biomedical research is now confirming the empirical insights of these traditions — the Tibetan concept of mKhris-pa correspondeth to inflammatory processes, the Mongolian Blood-Heat to circulatory hyperfunction, the Uyghur dry-heat to states of dehydration, the Miao toxicity to metabolic waste accumulation. The wisdom of the highlands, accumulated over centuries of observation and practice, is being vindicated by the laboratory sciences of our own age.

## Chapter IX. Southeast Asian Traditional Medicine

The torrid zone of Southeast Asia, where the monsoon rains nourish a luxuriance of vegetable life unequalled upon the globe, and where the influences of India and China have contended for two millennia, hath given birth to medical systems of singular interest. In the present chapter we shall examine the traditions of Thailand, Myanmar, Indonesia, Vietnam, Cambodia, and Laos, and demonstrate that their common character — a fusion of Indian, Chinese, and indigenous shamanic elements — hath produced a holonic medicine of remarkable diversity.

The Traditional Medicine of Thailand, known as Thai, is founded upon the doctrine of Four Elements, or Dhātu: Pathavi (Earth), governing the solid tissues; Apo (Water), presiding over the bodily fluids; Tejo (Fire), controlling temperature and digestion; and Vāyo (Wind), regulating respiration and movement. Disease ariseth from the imbalance of these four elements, and health is their perfect harmony. The Thai pharmacopoeia, comprising more than a thousand simples, draweth upon the extraordinary botanical wealth of the kingdom — turmeric, lemongrass, galangal, and Andrographis paniculata being among the most celebrated. But it is in the practice of Nuad Thai, or Thai massage, that the distinctive character of this tradition is most fully expressed. This system, recognised by UNESCO as an intangible cultural heritage in 2019, operateth upon ten principal energy lines, or Sen, which traverse the body in a manner analogous to the meridians of Chinese acupuncture. The practitioner, employing his thumbs, palms, elbows, knees, and feet, applies rhythmic pressure along these lines, stretching and manipulating the patient's body in a sequence of movements that hath been preserved in the stone reliefs of Wat Pho in Bangkok since 1832.

The Medicine of Myanmar, formerly Burma, recogniseth three humours — Wind, Bile, and Phlegm — derived from the Indian Tridosha. Its distinctive feature is the incorporation of astrological medicine, wherein eight celestial houses are correlated with the parts of the body, and the moment of birth is held to determine the constitutional type and disease predisposition of the individual. This is holonic reasoning of a particularly pure form: the macrocosm of the heavens is reflected in the microcosm of the human frame.

The Indonesian tradition of Jamu, which hath flourished for more than thirteen centuries, is perhaps the most democratically diffused of all Southeast Asian medical systems. The bas-reliefs of the Borobudur temple, erected in the eighth century of the Christian era, depict the preparation of herbal remedies, and the tradition hath been preserved, transmitted, and practised ever since, primarily by women — the Ibu Jamu, or Jamu Mothers. Among the classic formulations are Jamu Kunyit Asam, a decoction of turmeric and tamarind used for dysmenorrhea and weight management; Jamu Beras Kencur, a preparation of rice and galangal employed to stimulate the appetite; and Jamu Pahitan, a bitter infusion prescribed for febrile conditions. The modern Jamu industry of Indonesia is valued at more than three billion United States dollars annually, and the Ministry of Health hath formally recognised its place in the national healthcare system.

The Medicine of Vietnam, Thuốc Nam, we have already considered in its relationship to Chinese medicine. Its distinctive contribution is the systematic integration of Southern and Northern drugs — the tropical simples of Vietnam coexisting with the temperate pharmacopoeia of China. The great physician Hải Thượng Lãn Ông, in the eighteenth century, codified this synthesis in his monumental work, establishing a tradition that endureth to the present day.

The Medicine of Cambodia, or Khmer medicine, is predominantly shaped by Indian Ayurveda and Theravada Buddhism, with a particular expertise in the treatment of snake envenomation — a practical necessity in a land of numerous and dangerous serpents. The Medicine of Laos, closely related to that of Thailand, retaineth a stronger shamanic element, with the Hmong herbal tradition representing a distinct and valuable corpus of empirical knowledge.

If we survey the five principal systems of Southeast Asia — Thai, Burmese, Indonesian, Vietnamese, and Khmer — we discern a common structure beneath their apparent diversity. All are built upon a foundation of humoral theory derived from India; all have been overlaid with Chinese diagnostic and therapeutic methods; all have incorporated the indigenous shamanic traditions that preceded the arrival of both civilising influences; and all have adapted their pharmacopoeiae to the extraordinary botanical riches of the tropical zone. The holonic character of these systems is manifest: the energy lines of Thai massage, the astrological houses of Burmese medicine, the herbal formulations of Jamu, the Southern and Northern drugs of Vietnam — each represents a local expression that mirrorreth the whole, a part that containeth the image of the totality.

## Chapter X. Indigenous North American Medicine

The vast continent of North America, peopled for more than ten thousand years by nations of extraordinary diversity, hath developed medical traditions of profound spiritual and empirical depth. In the present chapter we shall examine the healing systems of the Cherokee, the Navajo, the Haudenosaunee, and the pan-tribal shamanic tradition, and demonstrate that their common foundation — the inseparability of body, mind, spirit, and cosmos — constituteth a holonic medicine of singular power.

The Shaman, that figure who reappeareth in every continent and every epoch, is the central institution of indigenous North American medicine. He is at once physician, priest, and psychologist, and his practice is founded upon the art of ecstasy — the capacity to enter altered states of consciousness wherein he may communicate with the spirit world. The methods by which this state is induced are various: the rhythmic beating of the drum, which modern neuroscience hath shown to alter brainwave patterns; the chanting of sacred songs; the ecstatic dance; and the ingestion of visionary plants, such as peyote and certain mushrooms. The shamanic theory of disease holdeth that illness ariseth from the loss of the soul or the invasion of malevolent spirits. The cure, consequently, consisteth in the shaman's spiritual journey to recover the lost soul or to expel the intruder — a procedure that presupposeth the capacity of the shaman's consciousness, a local phenomenon, to access and influence the whole of the patient's being.

The Cherokee nation, originally inhabiting the southeastern woodlands of the present United States, developed a sophisticated herbal pharmacopoeia. Among the most celebrated of their remedies are blackberry root for diarrhoea, bloodroot for cutaneous affections and oral ulcers, and echinacea — that now-ubiquitous immunostimulant whose annual global market exceeded nine hundred and fifty million dollars in 2018. The Cherokee recognise four sacred plants — tobacco, sage, sweetgrass, and cedar — which are employed in the smudging ceremony, wherein the smoke of these herbs is used to purify the body, the dwelling, and the ceremonial space. The inhalation of the smoke, a local act, effecteth a purification of the whole person.

The Navajo nation, of the arid Southwest, hath developed a healing tradition of extraordinary ceremonial complexity. The central practice is the Healing Ceremony, or Sing, which may continue for two to nine days and culminates in the creation of a sand painting. The medicine man, or Hatałii, draweth upon the earth a representation of the Holy People, using coloured sands meticulously applied. The patient is then placed upon this sacred image, and the power of the Holy People is held to pass into the sufferer, absorbing his disease and restoring his harmony with the cosmos. At the conclusion of the ceremony, the sand painting is erased, the disease having been transferred to the earth. This practice is, perhaps, the purest expression of holonic medicine to be found in any tradition: the sand painting is a local, material representation of the patient, and through the ritual, the part (the image) effecteth a transformation of the whole (the person). The Navajo also possess the beautiful doctrine of the Song of Life — the belief that each individual hath a unique life-song, and that disease ariseth when this song is interrupted, the cure consisting in the Hatałii's re-singing of the patient's song until it floweth once more without obstruction.

The Haudenosaunee, or Iroquois Confederacy, of the northeastern woodlands, have bequeathed to us a theory of the dual soul. The human person, in their conception, possesseth two souls: the True Self, which is elevated and authentic, and the Shadow Self, which is base and obscured. Disease ariseth when the True Self is eclipsed by the Shadow, and the cure consisteth in the medicine society's ceremonies, which restore the sufferer to his authentic nature. This theory, which recognisably anticipates the analytical psychology of Carl Gustav Jung, is a holonic psychology: the soul, a local aspect of the person, mirrorreth the whole personality.

The Sacred Pipe, or Chanunpa, is the central ritual instrument of many Plains nations. The pipe is understood as a bridge between the human and the divine; the tobacco smoke, rising heavenward, carrieth the prayers of the people to the Great Spirit. The pipe, a local object, connecteth the individual to the totality of the cosmos — a holonic sacrament.

The twelve principal medicinal plants of indigenous North America — tobacco, sage, sweetgrass, cedar, echinacea, blackberry, bloodroot, goldenrod, lavender, yarrow, eucalyptus, and others — constitute a pharmacopoeia of proven efficacy, several of which have entered the global market. The HoloScan 2.0, if it is to be truly comprehensive, must incorporate this knowledge, together with the shamanic diagnostic and therapeutic methods that give it context and meaning.

## Chapter XI. Latin American Traditional Medicine

The civilisations of Mesoamerica and the Andes, together with the shamanic traditions of the Amazon rainforest, constitute a third great pole of indigenous medical achievement, comparable in sophistication and antiquity to those of East Asia and the Mediterranean. In the present chapter we shall examine the medical systems of the Maya, the Aztecs, the Incas, and the Amazonian peoples, and demonstrate that their common foundation — the unity of the human, the natural, and the cosmic — is a holonic principle of the highest order.

The Maya civilisation, which flourished in the Yucatán Peninsula and Central America for more than two millennia, hath bequeathed to us, in the Dresden Codex and other surviving manuscripts, evidence of a medical system of remarkable refinement. The Maya conceived of health as a triple equilibrium — among the individual, the natural world, and the cosmos. Disease was the rupture of this equilibrium, and the cure consisted in its restoration through herbal remedies, ritual purification, and the propitiation of the gods. The Maya pharmacopoeia included chamomile for sedation, achiote for inflammation, copal for fumigation, and guayule for gout. The triple equilibrium of Maya medicine is, in its essentials, a holonic cosmology: the individual (the part) is a microcosm of nature and the cosmos (the whole), and the health of the part dependeth upon the harmony of the whole.

The Aztec Empire, which dominated central Mexico from the fourteenth to the sixteenth centuries, developed a system of botanical medicine of extraordinary sophistication. The Emperor Moctezuma II maintained vast botanical gardens — the famous chinampas, or floating gardens — wherein medicinal plants were cultivated and studied. The Aztec physicians, or ticitl, employed a pharmacopoeia that included chia seeds (now recognised as among the richest plant sources of omega-3 fatty acids), aloe vera (whose efficacy in burn treatment hath been confirmed by clinical trials), cacao (which containeth dopamine precursors and powerful antioxidants), vanilla, and tomato. It is a remarkable fact that the Aztecs, five centuries before the advent of modern nutritional science, had identified the most nutrientially valuable plants of their environment.

The Inca Empire, which stretched along the spine of the Andes from modern Colombia to Chile, developed a medical system founded upon a tripartite cosmology: Hanan Pacha, the upper world of the gods; Kay Pacha, the middle world of human existence; and Uku Pacha, the lower world of the dead and the unborn. Disease arose from the imbalance among these three worlds, and the cure consisted in the restoration of cosmic harmony. The Inca pharmacopoeia included coca, from which modern cocaine was first isolated in 1855 and whose local anaesthetic properties revolutionised surgery; cat's claw, whose anti-inflammatory effects have been confirmed by modern research; maca, used for sexual dysfunction; camu camu, among the richest known sources of vitamin C; and yerba mate, the stimulant beverage of the Southern Cone.

The Amazonian shamanic tradition, practiced by the numerous indigenous peoples of the great rainforest, is founded upon the sacramental use of ayahuasca — a decoction prepared from the Banisteriopsis caapi vine and the Psychotria viridis leaf. The ayahuasca ceremony, conducted by the shaman, or pajé, is a therapeutic procedure of extraordinary intensity. The patient ingesteth the bitter brew, which containeth dimethyltryptamine (DMT) and monoamine oxidase inhibitors, and under its influence entereth a state of heightened consciousness lasting four to six hours. The shaman, chanting the sacred icaros, guideth the patient's vision, perceiving the spiritual causes of the disease, entering the patient's body to extract the pathogenic essence, and ultimately restoring the sufferer to health. The United States Food and Drug Administration, in 2017, granted breakthrough therapy designation to ayahuasca for the treatment of post-traumatic stress disorder, depression, and substance addiction — a recognition of the therapeutic potential of this ancient Amazonian practice.

The holonic character of Latin American indigenous medicine is unmistakable. The Maya triple equilibrium, the Aztec botanical gardens, the Inca tripartite cosmology, and the Amazonian ayahuasca ceremony all express, in their various idioms, the same fundamental insight: that the part reflecteth the whole, that the local condition is a manifestation of the global state, and that healing must address not merely the symptom but the entire fabric of the patient's existence — physical, social, environmental, and cosmic.

## Chapter XII. African Traditional Medicine

The continent of Africa, the cradle of our species, hath nurtured medical traditions of extraordinary antiquity and diversity. In the present chapter we shall examine the principal systems of the continent — the legacy of ancient Egypt as transmitted through Nubia and Kush; the medicine of the Yoruba of West Africa; the healing practices of the San of the Kalahari; the Ethiopian Christian tradition; and the unique contributions of Madagascar — and demonstrate that each, in its own manner, constituteth a holonic medicine of profound insight.

The Medicine of Ancient Egypt, which flourished for three thousand years along the banks of the Nile, did not perish with the fall of the Pharaohs but was transmitted southward into Nubia and the Kingdom of Kush. Archaeological discoveries in the Sudan have revealed medical prescriptions from the second millennium before the Christian era that contain myrrh and aloe vera, and the dual role of physician and priest, characteristic of Egyptian civilisation, was perpetuated in the Nubian tradition. The holonic character of Egyptian medicine — the conception of the body as a system of correspondences, the practice of treating the whole through the part — thus passed into the deeper continent.

The Yoruba civilisation of present-day Nigeria and Benin developed a medical system of remarkable psychological sophistication. The central figure is the Babalawo, the father of secrets, who is at once diviner, physician, and priest. The diagnostic method is the Ifa divination system, a complex corpus of oral literature comprising two hundred and fifty-six principal odù, or verses, each containing multiple narratives and prescriptions. The theory of disease among the Yoruba is founded upon the doctrine of Three Souls: the Okan, or head-soul, governing thought; the Emi, or heart-soul, governing emotion; and the Ori Inu, or inner head, governing destiny. Disease ariseth from the imbalance of these three souls, and the cure consisteth in the restoration of their harmony through sacrifice (ebó), herbal medicine, and the recitation of the appropriate odù. The Yoruba pharmacopoeia includeth the moringa tree, which the United Nations hath recommended for combating malnutrition and which containeth ninety or more nutrients, including seven times the vitamin C of oranges and four times the calcium of milk. The bitter kola nut, employed for infections, and hibiscus, used for hypertension, are among the other notable simples.

The San people of the Kalahari Desert represent one of the most ancient continuous cultures on earth, and their medical tradition, stretching back tens of thousands of years, is of extraordinary interest. The San shaman, or N/om therapist, enters a state of profound trance — a condition that modern neuroscientific investigation hath shown to involve a ninety percent reduction in prefrontal cortical activity, a pattern resembling that of near-death experiences. In this state, the shaman's soul is believed to depart the body, travel to the spirit world, and there recover the patient's lost soul. The San pharmacopoeia includeth Hoodia gordonii, a succulent plant whose appetite-suppressing properties were investigated by pharmaceutical companies (though ethical controversies over the exploitation of San knowledge eventually led to the abandonment of these efforts); Devil's Claw, for inflammation; Buchu, also for inflammation; and Honeybush Tea, for its antioxidant properties.

The Ethiopian tradition, uniquely among African nations, is shaped by the ancient Christian faith of the country. Prayer, holy water, and herbal medicine are combined in a therapeutic system of distinctive character. The most remarkable contribution of Ethiopian medicine is the discovery of Krarobat, or Endod, a plant whose berries have been used for centuries to kill the snails that transmit schistosomiasis — a disease that afflicts more than two hundred million people in sub-Saharan Africa. Modern research hath confirmed the molluscicidal properties of this plant, and the World Health Organization hath expressed interest in its large-scale application.

The island of Madagascar, separated from the African mainland by the Mozambique Channel, developed a medical tradition of remarkable independence. The Malagasy conceive of health as the equilibrium of three principles: Zanahary, the creator god; Tany, the earth mother; and Olombelona, humanity. The most celebrated contribution of Madagascar to global medicine is the rosy periwinkle, Catharanthus roseus, from which the alkaloids vincristine and vinblastine were isolated — drugs that have transformed the treatment of childhood leukaemia and Hodgkin's lymphoma. This is the most successful example of the translation of indigenous botanical knowledge into modern pharmaceutical therapy: a traditional Malagasy remedy, employed for generations, yielded two of the most valuable drugs in the oncological armamentarium.

The African continent, with its five thousand years of continuous medical tradition, its extraordinary botanical diversity, and its sophisticated psychological and spiritual theories of disease, offers a rich repository of holonic wisdom. The Yoruba Three Souls, the San trance state, the Ethiopian Krarobat, the Madagascan periwinkle — each is a local expression that containeth the image of the whole, a part that could not be understood without reference to the totality of the tradition from which it emerged.

## Chapter XIII. Siberian, Mongolian, and Arctic Traditional Medicine

The northernmost regions of the habitable globe, where the temperature descendeth to fifty degrees below zero and the sun, in winter, scarcely riseth above the horizon, have given birth to a medical tradition of singular character. The shamanic complex of Siberia, Mongolia, and the Arctic, together with the empirical adaptations of the Inuit and other circumpolar peoples, constituteth a holonic medicine shaped by the most extreme environment in which humanity hath ever established a permanent existence.

The word "shaman" itself is derived from the Tungusic languages of Siberia, and this vast region — from the Urals to the Pacific, from the Arctic Ocean to the steppes of Mongolia — is the original homeland of the shamanic tradition. The Evenki, the Yakut, the Buryat, and numerous other peoples developed a complex cosmology centred upon the figure of the shaman, who is capable of traversing the three worlds of the cosmos: the Upper World of the sky, inhabited by ancestral spirits and gods; the Middle World of everyday human existence; and the Lower World beneath the earth, the realm of spirits and the dead. The shaman's drum, made of deerskin stretched over a circular frame, is the instrument of this celestial journey; the drumming, at a frequency of four to seven beats per second, induces a trance state wherein the shaman's soul is liberated from the body and embarks upon its voyage. The shaman's costume — a deerskin robe adorned with iron discs, bells, and a headdress of antlers — is a microcosm of the cosmic order, each element bearing a specific symbolic meaning. The iron discs represent thunder, with which the shaman drives away malevolent spirits; the bells serve as warnings; the antlers connect the shaman to the cosmic axis, the world tree that unites the three realms.

The Mongolian shamanic tradition, which we have already encountered in our discussion of Mongolian medicine (Chapter VIII), is distinguished by its relationship to the Tengri, or Sky God. The Mongol shaman, or Böö, employeth drumming, chanting, and the remarkable technique of Khoomei, or throat-singing, to enter trance and communicate with the celestial powers. The introduction of Tibetan Buddhism in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries led to the partial suppression of shamanic practices, but the tradition hath persisted among the common people and, in recent decades, hath experienced a notable revival.

The Inuit peoples, inhabiting the Arctic coasts of Canada, Alaska, Greenland, and Russia, developed a medical tradition adapted to the most extreme conditions on earth. The Inuit shaman, or Angakkuq, employed a distinctive therapeutic method known as Anger Treatment: the healer would perform an elaborate display of rage before the patient, shouting, striking, and leaping, with the intention of driving out the malevolent spirit that was believed to have caused the disease. The Inuit pharmacopoeia, limited by the sparse vegetation of the Arctic, is founded upon animal products: seal oil, rich in omega-3 fatty acids, which modern research hath shown to possess anti-inflammatory and cardioprotective properties; whale blubber, a concentrated source of vitamin D in a region where sunlight is insufficient for its endogenous synthesis; and the bark of the Arctic willow, which containeth salicin, the precursor of aspirin. The low incidence of cardiovascular disease among the Inuit, despite a diet exceedingly rich in animal fat, hath been attributed to these marine-derived nutrients — a confirmation of traditional wisdom by modern epidemiological science.

The Yakut people of northeastern Siberia, the most cold-adapted human population on earth, developed a shamanic tradition of particular elaboration. The Yakut shaman, or Yosutii, journeyed to the Upper World to consult the ancestral spirits and to the Lower World to do battle with the pathogenic forces. The Kamchatka Peninsula, at the far eastern extremity of Siberia, was inhabited by the Itelmen and Koryak peoples, whose shamanic tradition was shaped by the volcanic and geothermal activity of the region — the spirits of the volcanoes being propitiated for the health of the community.

The holonic character of Arctic and Siberian medicine is manifest in its cosmology. The shaman's drum, a local object, is the gateway to the three worlds; the shaman's costume, a local assemblage of skins and iron, is a microcosm of the universe; the shaman's soul, a local phenomenon, traverses the totality of existence. In the Inuit Anger Treatment, a local emotional expression — rage — effecteth a transformation of the whole person. The Arctic environment, in its extreme character, hath produced a medicine of extreme holonic clarity: the part and the whole are not merely analogous but are actively engaged in a dynamic relationship of mutual influence.

## Chapter XIV. Oceanian Traditional Medicine

We come now to the final chapter of this second volume, and to a medical tradition of unparalleled antiquity: the indigenous medicine of Australia, together with the traditions of the Pacific Islands. Here, upon the oldest continent and upon the countless islands of the greatest ocean, we find the longest continuous medical tradition in human history — a tradition extending back sixty-five thousand years — and a series of island medical systems that, in their isolation, developed holonic principles of remarkable purity.

The Aboriginal peoples of Australia, numbering more than a thousand distinct groups speaking more than two hundred and fifty languages, have maintained an unbroken cultural continuity for sixty-five millennia — quite possibly the longest continuous cultural tradition that our species hath produced. The foundation of this tradition is the Dreamtime, or Tjukurpa, a concept of such profundity that it eludeth easy translation. The Dreamtime is not a past epoch but a timeless dimension wherein the ancestral beings created the world — the mountains, the rivers, the animals, the plants — and wherein these beings continue to exist. Past, present, and future are simultaneous in the Dreamtime; the ancestral beings are ever-present, and the individual participate in their existence through ceremony, song, and the sacred geography of the land. Disease, in this conception, is a state of separation from the Dreamtime; health is the restoration of connection.

The Songlines, or Dreaming Tracks, are the pathways that traverse the Australian continent, each one a song that was sung by the ancestral beings as they created the land. To sing a songline is to traverse the continent in the spirit, to follow the path of the ancestors, to connect the local place where one stands to the entirety of the land. The songlines encode topographical information — the location of waterholes, the distribution of medicinal plants, the safe passages through difficult terrain — in a mnemonic system of extraordinary efficiency. The holonic character of the songlines is unmistakable: a song, a local acoustic phenomenon, containeth within itself the map of an entire continent.

The Aboriginal pharmacopoeia hath contributed several plants of global significance. The tea tree, Melaleuca alternifolia, whose essential oil possesseth powerful antibacterial and antifungal properties, hath become a billion-dollar global industry; its efficacy against methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) was confirmed by clinical research in the 1980s. The eucalyptus, whose oil is employed for respiratory conditions; the Kakadu plum, which containeth the highest concentration of vitamin C of any plant on earth — one hundred times that of the orange; lemon myrtle, with its antimicrobial properties; and wattleseed, a nutritious food source — these are the gifts of the world's oldest continuous medical tradition to global pharmacotherapy.

The Aboriginal healers, known as Ngangkari, practice a medicine that is at once physical, spiritual, and musical. The Ngangkari enter a trance state, apply pressure to specific points on the patient's body in a manner that recallleth the acupuncture points of Chinese medicine, chant the songlines appropriate to the patient's condition and ancestral lineage, and extract the pathogenic essence. In 2009, the Anangu Ngangkari Tjutaku Aboriginal Corporation was established, and since 2018, the Ngangkari have been formally integrated into Western hospitals in certain regions of Australia — a collaboration that representeth a model for the integration of indigenous and biomedical knowledge.

The Hawaiian tradition of Lomilomi, which hath persisted for more than four thousand years, is a system of deep-tissue massage that is inseparable from the Huna spiritual tradition. The practitioner, employing the forearms and palms (not the fingertips) in a wave-like motion that imitateth the movement of the ocean, accompanied by prayer and chant, treateth not merely the body but the whole person — physical, mental, and spiritual. The comparison with Chinese tuina massage is instructive: both systems operate through physical contact to influence the whole organism, but Lomilomi, with its oceanic rhythms and its integration of prayer, is a distinctively Polynesian expression of the holonic principle.

The Māori of New Zealand developed the tradition of Rongoā, which encompasseth herbal medicine, the massage technique of mirimiri, and spiritual healing. The Māori pharmacopoeia includeth kawakawa, for inflammation and gastric disorders; koromiko, for diarrhoea and wounds; and, most famously, the mānuka tree, whose honey hath been found to contain methylglyoxal, a compound of exceptional antibacterial potency. The international market for mānuka honey now exceedeth five hundred million dollars annually, and its efficacy in wound healing, cutaneous infections, and oral ulcers hath been extensively documented. The Māori theory of health is founded upon the doctrine of Three Souls: Wairua, the soul from the ancestors; Mauri, the life force from the ancestral spirits; and Tapu, the sacred power connected to mana. Disease ariseth from the imbalance of these three principles, and the cure consisteth in their restoration through ritual, herbal medicine, and the lifting of tapu.

The wider Polynesian tradition, encompassing Samoa, Tonga, the Cook Islands, and many others, contributed the celebrated kava — a beverage prepared from the root of Piper methysticum, whose sedative and anxiolytic properties have been confirmed by modern research. The kavalactones, the active principles of kava, act upon the GABA and dopamine systems of the brain, and for a period in the 1990s and early 2000s, the international market for kava products exceeded half a billion dollars annually.

As we close this second volume, let us cast our gaze backward over the ground we have traversed. In eight chapters, we have surveyed the medical traditions of six continents and more than fifty indigenous peoples, from the high plateau of Tibet to the rainforests of the Amazon, from the Arctic Circle to the islands of the Pacific, from the savannahs of Africa to the deserts of Australia. Everywhere we have found the same fundamental principle, independently discovered and independently developed: the part reflecteth the whole; the local condition is a manifestation of the global state; the individual is a microcosm of the cosmos.

The holonic embryo is not the invention of any single civilisation. It is a universal principle of human medicine, discovered again and again by peoples who could have had no communication with one another, because it is a truth inscribed in the very structure of living organisms. The acupuncture point, the Tibetan urine, the Navajo sand painting, the Australian songline — each is a local expression that containeth the image of the totality, a part that could not be understood without the whole, a whole that cannot exist without the parts.

Volume II of this work hath thus demonstrated, through the accumulated evidence of fifty or more indigenous medical traditions, distributed across the entire habitable globe, that the holonic principle is not a cultural artefact but a natural law of medical thought. Volume III will examine the scientific foundations of this principle in the modern disciplines of neuroscience, molecular biology, embryology, information theory, and physics.
