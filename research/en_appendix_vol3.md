### Section 16.1. The Holography of DNA

In the course of a human lifetime, some ten quadrillion cell divisions occur; the total number of mutations accumulated is perhaps ten million. The correspondence between the somites and the spinal nerves is exact: each pair of somites gives rise to a pair of spinal nerves, and the territories of these nerves — the dermatome, myotome, and viscerotome of the previous chapter — are the adult expressions of the embryonic somites. The forty-four pairs of somites give rise to the thirty-three vertebrae (seven cervical, twelve thoracic, five lumbar, five sacral, and four coccygeal, with the sacral and coccygeal fusing into single bones) and their associated nerves.

The significance of the somite for the holographic theory is this: the body is not assembled from a uniform block of tissue, but is segmented from the very beginning. Each somite contains within itself the information necessary to produce a complete segment of the body — nerves, bones, muscles, and skin. Each somite is, in the strictest sense, a holographic embryo.

The molecular mechanism of somitogenesis is a marvel of biological engineering. A "segmentation clock" — oscillations in the expression of the Hes/Her genes, with a period of approximately thirty minutes in the human embryo — governs the periodic formation of somites. The Notch signalling pathway maintains the clock; the Wnt and FGF pathways establish anterior and posterior boundaries; and the Hox genes assign to each somite its unique identity along the anterior-posterior axis.

### Section 18.1. The Foundations of Information Theory

Claude Elwood Shannon (1916–2001), a mathematician of extraordinary originality, published in the year 1948 his treatise "A Mathematical Theory of Communication," which gave birth to the science of information theory. The central insight of this work — that information can be quantified in bits, that communication channels have finite capacities, and that encoding can both compress and correct errors — has transformed every field of human knowledge, from telecommunications to genetics, and from cryptography to biology.

The fundamental quantities of information theory are these: the information content, measured in bits, is the negative logarithm of the probability of an event: $H = -\sum p \log_2 p$. The entropy $H(X)$ is the average uncertainty of a random variable. The channel capacity $C$ is the maximum mutual information between the input and output of a channel: $C = \max I(X;Y)$. The signal-to-noise ratio, or SNR, is the ratio of the signal power to the noise power. The mutual information $I(X;Y)$ measures the reduction in uncertainty about $X$ given knowledge of $Y$.

The Shannon-Hartley theorem — the most celebrated result in information theory — states that the capacity of a channel of bandwidth $B$ and signal-to-noise ratio $\text{SNR}$ is:

$$C = B \log_2 (1 + \text{SNR})$$

This equation, simple as it appears, contains the whole of the holographic theory in mathematical form. The body (the whole) is the information source; the microsystem (the part) is the noisy channel; the observation of the eight microsystems is the channel measurement; the multi-modal fusion is the combination of channels; and the digital twin is the maximum-likelihood estimate of the state of the body.

## Chapter XXI. Biokinetics and Pharmacokinetics

The science of drugs — their absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion; their interaction with targets; their toxicity at high doses and their efficacy at low ones — is the bridge between the holographic theory and the practice of medicine. ### Section 21.1. Pharmacokinetics: The Journey of the Drug

Pharmacokinetics — from the Greek *pharmakon* ("drug") and *kinesis* ("movement") — is the study of the fate of a drug in the body. The acronym ADME — Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion — encapsulates the four principal phases of this journey.

The key parameters of pharmacokinetics are the following: the peak concentration ($C_{\text{max}}$), the time to peak concentration ($T_{\text{max}}$), the area under the concentration-time curve (AUC), the half-life ($t_{1/2}$), the clearance ($CL$), the volume of distribution ($V_d$), and the bioavailability ($F$). The fundamental relationships are:

$$C_{\text{ss}} = \frac{D \times F}{V_d} \quad \text{(steady-state concentration)}$$

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{0.693 \times V_d}{CL} \quad \text{(half-life)}$$\n
$$\text{AUC} = \frac{F \times D}{CL} \quad \text{(total exposure)}$$

The clinical significance of these parameters is immediate. The dose of a drug is determined by the target concentration and the volume of distribution. The dosing interval is determined by the half-life: a drug with a short half-life must be given frequently; a drug with a long half-life may be given once daily. The route of administration — oral, intravenous, topical — is determined by the bioavailability. Special populations — the elderly, children, patients with hepatic or renal impairment — require adjustment of the dose based on changes in clearance and volume of distribution.

Consider three illustrative examples. Aspirin, that most ancient of drugs, has an oral bioavailability of seventy per cent, a half-life of twenty minutes for the parent compound and two to three hours for salicylic acid, and is given at a dose of seventy-five to one hundred milligrams daily for the prevention of cardiovascular and cerebrovascular disease. Penicillin, the first of the antibiotics, has an oral bioavailability of only thirty per cent, a half-life of thirty minutes, and must be given three to four times daily. Digoxin, the cardiac glycoside, has an oral bioavailability of seventy-five per cent, a half-life of thirty-six to forty-eight hours, and is given once daily. These differences in pharmacokinetics dictate the differences in clinical practice.

### Section 21.2. Pharmacodynamics: The Drug and Its Target

If pharmacokinetics describes what the body does to the drug, pharmacodynamics describes what the drug does to the body. The central concepts are these: the affinity of the drug for its target (measured by the dissociation constant $K_D$), the efficacy of the drug (the maximum effect $E_{\text{max}}$), and the potency of the drug (the concentration required to produce half the maximum effect, $\text{EC}_{50}$).

A drug may be an agonist, which activates its receptor; an antagonist, which blocks the receptor; a partial agonist, which produces a submaximal response; or an inverse agonist, which suppresses the basal activity of the receptor.

The classic examples of pharmacodynamics are drawn from the adrenergic and opioid systems. The beta-2 agonist salbutamol dilates the bronchial passages in asthma; the beta-blocker propranolol slows the heart in hypertension; the partial beta-agonist pindolol produces an intermediate effect. On the opioid axis, morphine is a full mu-agonist producing powerful analgesia; buprenorphine is a partial mu-agonist producing moderate analgesia with a ceiling effect; naloxone is a mu-antagonist used to reverse opioid overdose; and pentazocine is a kappa-agonist producing analgesia with a lower risk of addiction.

The enzyme inhibitors constitute another important class. The ACE inhibitors (captopril and its congeners) lower blood pressure; the HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors (the statins) lower cholesterol; the COX-2 inhibitors (celecoxib) reduce inflammation; and the PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil) treat erectile dysfunction. Each drug binds to its target with a specific affinity; each produces a specific effect; and each effect — local at the molecular level — propagates through the body as a holographic change.

Biokinetics — a term broader than pharmacokinetics — encompasses the fate and effects of all biologically active substances in the body: pharmaceutical drugs, herbal active ingredients, nutrients, endogenous substances (hormones, neurotransmitters), toxins, and nanoparticles.

### Section 21.7. Personalised Pharmacokinetics

The same dose of the same drug, given to two patients of the same age and weight, may produce very different concentrations and effects. The explanation lies in the genetic, physiological, and environmental factors that individualise pharmacokinetics.

The cytochrome P450 enzymes — the principal drug-metabolising enzymes of the liver — are highly polymorphic. CYP2D6, which metabolises more than twenty-five per cent of commonly used drugs, including many antidepressants, antipsychotics, beta-blockers, and opioids, exists in more than one hundred variant alleles. The poor metaboliser (PM), who carries two non-functional alleles, accumulates drugs to toxic concentrations. The ultrarapid metaboliser (UM), who carries multiple functional copies of the gene, clears drugs so quickly that they never reach therapeutic concentrations. The intermediate metaboliser (IM) and the extensive metaboliser (EM) lie between these extremes.

The implications for clinical practice are profound. ### Section 21.8. Toxicology: The Dose Makes the Poison

The toxicology of drugs and herbs — the study of their harmful effects — is the necessary complement to their pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics. The sixteenth-century physician Paracelsus, with characteristic bluntness, declared that "the dose makes the poison" — *dosis sola facit venenum* — a principle that remains the foundation of toxicology to this day.

The key concepts of toxicology are the median lethal dose ($\text{LD}_{50}$), the median toxic dose ($\text{TD}_{50}$), the no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL), and the lowest-observed-adverse-effect level (LOAEL).

The toxicology of Chinese herbal medicines is a subject of particular importance. Aconite (*Aconitum carmichaelii*, or *fu zi*), one of the most important herbs in the Chinese pharmacopoeia, contains aconitine, a potent neurotoxin with a median lethal dose of approximately 1.8 milligrams per kilogram in the rat. The traditional processing of aconite — prolonged boiling or steaming — hydrolyses the aconitine into less toxic derivatives, reducing the toxicity by a factor of two hundred. The processing of aconite is a masterpiece of empirical toxicology: the ancient Chinese physicians, without the instruments of modern science, discovered a method of rendering a deadly poison into a safe and effective medicine.

Other examples of herbal toxicity include aristolochic acid, from *Aristolochia* species (*ma dou ling*), a potent carcinogen that causes urothelial carcinoma and renal failure; triptolide, from *Tripterygium wilfordii* (*lei gong teng*), a hepatotoxin of considerable potency; and the anthraquinones of *Polygonum multiflorum* (*he shou wu*), which cause hepatotoxicity in susceptible individuals.

Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM) — the direct measurement of drug concentrations in the blood — is the most precise tool of personalised toxicology. It is indicated for drugs with a narrow therapeutic index, including digoxin (therapeutic range 0.5–2 nanograms per millilitre), lithium (0.6–1.2 milliequivalents per litre), warfarin (INR 2–3), theophylline (10–20 micrograms per millilitre), and the immunosuppressants tacrolimus and sirolimus.
