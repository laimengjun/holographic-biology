## Chapter LVI. Of the Therapeutic Systems of Traditional Chinese Medicine, and the Twenty-Seven Modalities of Holographic Treatment

The therapeutic systems of traditional Chinese medicine, as we have surveyed them in an earlier chapter, encompass sixty-three distinct modalities. The present chapter is devoted to a more detailed examination of the twenty-seven modalities that are most directly based on the holographic principle — the modalities that either explicitly invoke the holographic correspondence between the surface of the body and its internal organs, or that can be understood as applications of this correspondence.

The first and most fundamental of the holographic therapeutic modalities is acupuncture — the insertion of fine needles into specific points on the surface of the body for the purpose of influencing the function of the corresponding internal organs. Acupuncture is the direct clinical application of the holographic map: the acupuncturist, by stimulating a point on the surface, modulates the activity of the neural channel that connects that point to its corresponding organ, and thereby influences the state of the organ. The therapeutic effects of acupuncture have been confirmed by hundreds of clinical studies, and the mechanism of action — the modulation of neural activity through the stimulation of sensory nerves — is increasingly well understood.

The second modality is moxibustion — the burning of the dried leaves of the mugwort plant (Artemisia vulgaris) at specific points on the surface of the body. Moxibustion, like acupuncture, stimulates the holographic points, but it does so through the application of heat rather than through mechanical stimulation. The heat from the burning moxa penetrates the skin and activates the sensory nerves of the underlying tissues, producing effects that are similar to those of acupuncture but of longer duration.

The third modality is cupping — the application of heated cups to the surface of the body, creating a vacuum that draws the skin and the underlying tissues upward. Cupping stimulates the holographic points through a combination of mechanical stretching and increased blood flow, and it is used in the treatment of a wide range of conditions, from musculoskeletal pain to respiratory disorders.

The fourth modality is gua sha — the scraping of the skin with a smooth-edged instrument, producing petechiae — small red spots — that indicate the release of blood stagnation from the underlying tissues. Gua sha is used in the treatment of conditions that are characterized by blood stasis, such as chronic pain, menstrual disorders, and inflammatory conditions.

The fifth modality is tui na — Chinese therapeutic massage — which employs a variety of manual techniques — pressing, kneading, rolling, tapping, and stretching — to stimulate the holographic points and to correct the biomechanical imbalances that contribute to disease.

The sixth modality is qigong — a system of breathing exercises, meditative movements, and postural adjustments that is designed to cultivate and to regulate the flow of Qi — the vital energy — through the body. Qigong, like acupuncture, is based on the holographic principle: the movements and the postures of qigong are designed to stimulate the holographic points and to restore the balance of the organ system.

The seventh modality is tai chi — a martial art that is practised as a form of moving meditation, and that is believed to promote health by harmonizing the flow of Qi through the body. Tai chi, like qigong, is based on the holographic principle: the slow, graceful movements of tai chi are designed to stimulate the holographic points and to restore the balance of the organ system.

The remaining twenty modalities of the holographic therapeutic system include the various forms of herbal medicine that are prescribed according to the holographic diagnosis — the herbal formulas that are designed to restore the balance of the organs that the holographic examination has identified as disturbed; the dietary therapies that are prescribed according to the holographic constitution of the patient; the exercise therapies that are designed to strengthen the organs that the holographic examination has identified as weak; and the lifestyle modifications that are recommended to prevent the recurrence of the conditions that the holographic examination has identified as latent.

The twenty-seven modalities of holographic treatment, taken together, constitute a comprehensive system of therapy — a system that addresses the physical, the emotional, the dietary, and the behavioural dimensions of disease, and that is tailored to the individual holographic constitution of each patient. The HoloScan 2.0, by providing a quantitative assessment of the holographic state of the patient, can guide the selection of the most appropriate modalities and the most effective points of treatment.

## Chapter LVII. Of the Modern Medical Modalities That Embody the Holographic Principle, and the Convergence of East and West

The holographic principle, as we have argued throughout this treatise, is not the exclusive property of traditional Chinese medicine. It is a universal principle of biological organization, and it is embodied, whether consciously or unconsciously, in many of the therapeutic modalities of modern Western medicine. The present chapter is devoted to an examination of the modern medical modalities that embody the holographic principle, and to the demonstration that the convergence of Eastern and Western medicine is a convergence upon a common holographic foundation.

The first of the modern modalities that embodies the holographic principle is physical therapy — the branch of Western medicine that employs exercise, manual therapy, and physical agents such as heat, cold, and electricity to restore function and to relieve pain. The physical therapist, like the Chinese practitioner of tui na, treats the body as a biomechanical system in which the parts are connected to the whole, and in which a dysfunction in one part can produce symptoms in another.

The second modality is osteopathic medicine — a system of Western medicine that places particular emphasis on the musculoskeletal system and its relationship to the other systems of the body. The osteopathic physician, like the Chinese practitioner of bone setting, believes that the structure of the body influences its function, and that the correction of structural abnormalities — the realignment of the bones, the release of the fascia, the normalization of the joint motion — can restore the health of the whole organism.

The third modality is chiropractic — a system of Western medicine that focuses on the relationship between the spine and the nervous system, and on the correction of spinal misalignments — the subluxations — that are believed to interfere with the function of the nerves and to produce disease in the organs that these nerves supply. The chiropractor, like the Chinese acupuncturist, treats the surface of the body — the spine — to influence the function of the internal organs.

The fourth modality is biofeedback — a technique by which the patient learns to control physiological processes — heart rate, blood pressure, muscle tension, skin temperature — that are normally involuntary. The biofeedback therapist, like the Chinese qigong master, teaches the patient to regulate the internal state of his body by attending to the signals that the body produces.

The fifth modality is neurofeedback — a form of biofeedback that focuses on the electrical activity of the brain. The neurofeedback therapist, like the Chinese practitioner of acupuncture at the points of the scalp, stimulates specific regions of the brain to influence the function of the organs and the systems that these regions control.

The sixth modality is transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation — the application of electrical current to the surface of the body for the relief of pain. TENS, like acupuncture, stimulates the sensory nerves of the skin to modulate the activity of the underlying neural circuits, and it is used in the treatment of conditions that range from acute injury to chronic pain.

The seventh modality is vagus nerve stimulation — the application of electrical current to the vagus nerve, which connects the brain to the internal organs of the chest and the abdomen. Vagus nerve stimulation, like the stimulation of the auricular branch of the vagus nerve in ear acupuncture, influences the function of the internal organs by modulating the activity of the autonomic nervous system.

The convergence of Eastern and Western medicine upon these common modalities is not a coincidence; it is a convergence upon the holographic principle. The Eastern traditions discovered the holographic principle through millennia of clinical observation; the Western traditions are rediscovering it through the application of modern science. The two traditions are converging, not because one is imitating the other, but because both are responding to the same underlying reality — the holographic organisation of the living organism.
