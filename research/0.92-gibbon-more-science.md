## Chapter LI. Of the Molecular Evidence for the Holographic Principle, and the DNA That Every Cell Contains

The proposition that every part of the living organism contains the information of the whole — the proposition upon which the entire edifice of holographic diagnosis rests — finds its most compelling confirmation in the molecular biology of the DNA molecule. Every cell of the human body, with the exception of the red blood cells which have lost their nuclei and the gametes which have reduced their genetic complement by half, contains a complete copy of the human genome — a molecule of DNA approximately two metres in length, comprising three billion base pairs, encoding the information required to construct and to maintain the entire organism.

The significance of this fact for the holographic theory can scarcely be overstated. The DNA molecule is, in a very literal sense, a hologram: every nucleated cell of the body contains the same genetic information, and this information is the blueprint of the whole organism. The skin cell of the finger and the liver cell of the abdomen contain the same genes; they differ not in the information they possess but in the information they express. The holographic principle — that every part contains the information of the whole — is not a metaphor but a molecular fact, inscribed in the double helix of every cell nucleus.

The total quantity of DNA in the human body is staggering. A single cell contains six picograms of DNA; the adult human body contains approximately thirty-seven trillion cells; the total mass of DNA in the body is therefore approximately two hundred and twenty grams. If the DNA molecules of all the cells of a single human body were laid end to end, they would stretch for approximately twenty billion kilometres — a distance that would reach from the Earth to the outer limits of the solar system and back again, fifty thousand times. The information storage capacity of this DNA is approximately sixty billion gigabytes — a figure that exceeds the total digital information storage capacity of all the computers on Earth.

The fidelity with which this information is replicated during cell division is remarkable. The DNA polymerase, the enzyme that copies the DNA molecule, has an error rate of approximately one mistake per hundred thousand base pairs — a figure that sounds alarming until one learns that the polymerase also possesses a proofreading function, a molecular editor that checks the newly synthesized strand for errors and corrects them. The combined error rate of the polymerase and its proofreader is approximately one mistake per ten million base pairs. A third layer of error correction — the mismatch repair system — reduces the error rate still further, to approximately one mistake per billion base pairs.

This error rate of one in a billion is the fundamental constant of biological information transfer. It determines the fidelity with which genetic information is transmitted from one generation of cells to the next; it determines the rate at which mutations accumulate in the cells of the body; and it determines, ultimately, the limits of holographic diagnosis. If the error rate were much higher, the holographic information would be degraded by the accumulation of mutations, and the correspondence between the surface of the body and its internal organs would be lost. If the error rate were much lower, evolution would be impossible, and the diversity of life would never have arisen. The error rate of DNA replication is, in a very real sense, the tuning parameter of the holographic universe — the knob that balances the competing demands of fidelity and variation, of stability and change.

## Chapter LII. Of the Embryological Evidence for the Holographic Principle, and the Development of the Body Plan

The embryological evidence for the holographic principle is, if possible, even more compelling than the molecular evidence. The development of a human being from a single fertilized cell to an organism of thirty-seven trillion cells is a process that can only be described as holographic: every stage of development recapitulates the whole, and every cell contains the potential to become any part of the whole.

The first few divisions of the fertilized egg produce a cluster of cells — the morula — that are still totipotent: each cell, if separated from the others, can develop into a complete embryo. This is the holographic principle in its purest form: every part contains the information of the whole, and the whole can be reconstructed from any of its parts. The cells of the early embryo are not yet specialized; they are pluripotent, capable of becoming any cell type of the adult organism. The information required to construct the entire body is present in each cell, and the fate of each cell is determined not by its intrinsic properties but by its position within the developing embryo.

As development proceeds, the cells of the embryo begin to differentiate — to specialize, to acquire the distinctive characteristics of the cell types that they will become. The process of differentiation is governed by the expression of specific genes, which are activated or repressed in response to signals from the surrounding cells. The Hox genes, which we have described in an earlier chapter, play a central role in this process: they determine the identity of each segment of the embryo along the head-to-tail axis, and they ensure that each segment develops the appropriate structures.

The process of differentiation does not destroy the holographic information that each cell contains. Even after a cell has differentiated into a skin cell, a liver cell, or a nerve cell, it retains the complete genome of the organism. The skin cell has the genes for making liver enzymes; the liver cell has the genes for making skin proteins; the nerve cell has the genes for making red blood cells. These genes are not expressed — they are silenced by the mechanisms of epigenetic regulation — but they are present, and they can be reactivated under appropriate conditions, as the cloning experiments of the late twentieth century have demonstrated.

The cloning of Dolly the sheep, announced to the world in 1997, was the most dramatic demonstration of the holographic principle in the modern era. Dolly was created from a single cell taken from the mammary gland of an adult sheep — a cell that had already differentiated, that had already acquired the specialised characteristics of a mammary epithelial cell. Yet this cell, when placed in an enucleated egg and stimulated to divide, gave rise to a complete organism — a lamb that was genetically identical to the sheep from which the cell had been taken. The differentiated cell, despite its specialization, contained the information required to construct the whole organism. The holographic principle was confirmed, not by the observation of an ancient clinical tradition, but by the most rigorous methods of modern molecular biology.

The embryological evidence for the holographic principle is thus overwhelming. The development of the embryo is a holographic process, in which every part contains the information of the whole, and in which the whole emerges from the interaction of the parts. The cloning experiments of the modern era have confirmed, with a precision that leaves no room for doubt, that the information required to construct the whole organism is present in every cell — a fact that the Chinese physicians had intuited, two thousand years before the birth of modern biology, when they mapped the holographic correspondences of the human body.

## Chapter LIII. Of the Biomechanics of the Holographic System, and the Physical Forces That Shape the Living Form

The holographic organisation of the living organism is not solely a matter of genes and molecules; it is also a matter of physics — of the forces that shape the living form, and of the mechanical properties of the tissues that determine how these forces are transmitted from one part of the body to another. The present chapter is devoted to the biomechanics of the holographic system, and to the demonstration that the physical forces acting on the body play an essential role in maintaining the holographic correspondences.

The simplest and most familiar example of biomechanical holography is the relationship between the foot and the spine. The foot, when it strikes the ground during walking, transmits a force that is propagated through the bones of the leg, the pelvis, and the spine to the skull. The nature of this force transmission depends on the structure of the foot: a fallen arch, a hammer toe, or a bunion can alter the pattern of force transmission and produce characteristic changes in the alignment of the pelvis and the curvature of the spine. The chiropractor or the osteopath, by examining the foot, can infer the condition of the spine; and by treating the foot, can influence the spine. This is holographic diagnosis and therapy, mediated not by nerves or by blood vessels but by the physical forces that connect the parts of the body.

A second example of biomechanical holography is the relationship between the pelvis and the organs of the abdomen. The pelvis, which forms the bony foundation of the trunk, supports the weight of the abdominal organs and provides the attachment points for the muscles that control their position. A misalignment of the pelvis — a torsion, a tilt, a rotation — can alter the position of the abdominal organs, compress the nerves that supply them, and impair their function. The osteopath, by examining the position of the pelvis, can infer the condition of the abdominal organs; and by adjusting the pelvis, can influence their function.

A third example is the relationship between the temporomandibular joint — the joint of the jaw — and the rest of the body. The temporomandibular joint is one of the most complex joints in the body, and its position and function are influenced by the alignment of the teeth, the tension of the muscles of mastication, and the posture of the head and the neck. A misalignment of the jaw can produce symptoms that appear to be unrelated: headaches, neck pain, back pain, dizziness, tinnitus. The dentist or the maxillofacial specialist who treats the jaw can, in many cases, alleviate these apparently unrelated symptoms. This is holographic therapy, mediated by the mechanical connections between the bones, the muscles, and the fascia.

The fascia — the connective tissue that surrounds and permeates every organ, every muscle, every bone, every nerve, every blood vessel of the body — is the most extensive organ of the body, and it is the medium through which the biomechanical holographic correspondences are transmitted. The fascia forms a continuous network that connects every part of the body to every other part; a tension in one region of the fascia is transmitted, through the network, to every other region. The fascial network is, in effect, a mechanical hologram: every point of the fascia is connected to every other point, and the state of the whole can be inferred from the state of any of its parts.

The biomechanics of the holographic system is thus an essential complement to the neural and the molecular foundations that we have described in earlier chapters. The neural foundation provides the informational channel; the molecular foundation provides the genetic program; and the biomechanical foundation provides the physical medium through which the forces that shape and maintain the holographic correspondences are transmitted. The holographic organism is not merely a neural network, nor merely a genetic program, nor merely a mechanical structure; it is all three, integrated into a single, unified whole.

## Chapter LV. Of the Integration of Holographic Diagnosis with the Technologies of the Modern Age, and the Design of the Clinical Trial

The theoretical framework of holographic diagnosis that we have developed in this treatise, and the engineering design of the HoloScan 2.0 that embodies it, have been presented as a complete system — a system that extends from the first principles of molecular biology to the practical design of a diagnostic instrument. But a system, however elegant in its conception, is worthless unless it can be validated by the methods of empirical science. The present chapter is devoted to the design of the clinical trial that would validate the holographic framework and the HoloScan 2.0.

The clinical trial should be designed as a prospective, multi-centre, blinded study, in which the diagnostic accuracy of the HoloScan 2.0 is compared with that of conventional diagnostic methods. The study population should consist of patients presenting to primary care clinics with a range of symptoms — abdominal pain, chest discomfort, headache, fatigue — that are common in general practice and that require diagnostic evaluation. The sample size should be calculated to detect a clinically significant difference in diagnostic accuracy between the HoloScan 2.0 and conventional methods, with a power of eighty percent and a significance level of five percent.

The protocol of the trial should be as follows. Each patient who consents to participate will undergo a complete history and physical examination by a physician trained in conventional Western medicine, who will record his diagnostic impressions and his recommendations for further investigation. The patient will then undergo an examination by the HoloScan 2.0, conducted by a technician who is blinded to the results of the conventional examination. The HoloScan 2.0 will record its diagnostic conclusions — the posterior probability of disease for each of the principal organs of the body — and these conclusions will be compared with the results of the conventional examination.

The gold standard against which both the conventional examination and the HoloScan 2.0 will be compared will be the results of the laboratory tests, imaging studies, and other investigations that are ordered as part of the patient's clinical care. The diagnostic accuracy of the HoloScan 2.0 will be assessed by calculating its sensitivity, specificity, positive predictive value, and negative predictive value for each of the conditions that are identified by the gold standard.

The trial should be conducted at multiple centres, in different geographical regions, to ensure that the results are generalizable. The centres should include both hospitals of Western medicine and hospitals of traditional Chinese medicine, to allow for a comparison of the HoloScan 2.0's performance in different clinical contexts. The trial should be overseen by an independent data monitoring committee, and the results should be published in a peer-reviewed journal, regardless of whether they are favourable or unfavourable to the holographic framework.

The clinical trial that I have described is not a theoretical exercise; it is a practical necessity. The holographic framework and the HoloScan 2.0 cannot be accepted as valid diagnostic tools until they have been tested by the most rigorous methods of clinical science. The trial that I have proposed would provide the evidence that is needed to establish the holographic framework on a secure empirical foundation.
