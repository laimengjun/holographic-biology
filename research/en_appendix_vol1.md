### § 1.1. Of the Sumerian Medical Tablets (circa 3000 BCE)

About the thirty-fifth century before the Christian era, the Sumerians established among the valleys of the Tigris and Euphrates (in that region which we now term Southern Iraq) the earliest urban civilisation known to history — the cities of Uruk, Ur, Lagash, and Nippur. Among the manifold consequences of urbanisation was the division of labour: beside the priests, the artisans, the merchants, and the husbandmen, there now appeared the first generation of men who devoted themselves wholly to the art of healing.

The cuneiform script of the Sumerians (invented about the year 3200 before our era) was among the earliest mature systems of writing known to mankind, conferring upon their medicine the inestimable advantage of **trans-generational record** — an advantage which other early civilisations (those of Egypt, India, and China) did not equally possess.

The Sumerian tablets have preserved for us a vast body of medical literature, which we may divide into three principal classes: the **therapeutic tablets**, containing herbal prescriptions, incantations, and rituals; the **diagnostic tablets**, wherein symptoms are described and prognoses delivered; and the **omen tablets**, by which diseases were foretold from the observation of natural phenomena.

**Of the Nippur Tablet (circa 2500 BCE).** Discovered in the year 1900 by the archaeological expedition of the University of Pennsylvania at Nippur, this tablet is among the most ancient medical documents yet brought to light. It contains no fewer than twelve prescriptions, mingling together herbal simples (such as myrrh and cypress), mineral substances (alum and bitumen), animal products (serpent-skin and tortoise-shell), and incantations of the form: "O Earth-god, remove his malady!"

We may observe with particular interest that the Nippur tablet already exhibits that **dual prescription paradigm** of "herb plus incantation" which bears a striking resemblance to the later traditions of Chinese medicine (herb plus meridian) and of Ayurveda (herb plus mantra).

**Of the Ashmolean Tablet (circa 2200 BCE).** Preserved in the Ashmolean Museum at Oxford, this tablet contains some thirty prescriptions, each following a standardised format: first, the patient's symptoms ("If the patient's head be hot and his eyes be bloodshot"); second, the proposed treatment ("Grind cypress bark, mix with beer, and apply to the forehead"); and third, the incantation ("I am the son of Anu, never shall disease conquer me").

This standardisation indicates that the Sumerian physicians had already instituted a system of **professional training** and a **repository of clinical cases**, not unlike the SOAP records of modern medicine.

**Of the "Three Hands" Tablet (circa 2100 BCE).** Preserved at the University of Pennsylvania Museum in Philadelphia, this tablet describes seven therapeutic prescriptions. Its peculiar distinction lies in the introduction of **palpatory diagnosis** — the physician pressing with his fingers upon various parts of the patient's body to determine the seat and nature of the malady.

From the perspective of the Holographic Embryo, this palpatory diagnosis embodies the very proposition that "the part reflects the whole" — the same proposition which Zhang Yingqing was to make the cornerstone of his holographic theory four thousand years thereafter. The Sumerian physician of five thousand years since was already performing, with his fingers, what we should now call a holographic examination.

The **Āsu** (herbalist or physician) derived his knowledge from folk wisdom and the apothecary's art, employing herbs, minerals, and animal medicaments to address natural causes of disease.

### § 1.4. Of the Babylonian Hepatoscopy

The most distinctive of all Babylonian medical practices was **hepatoscopy** — the art of foretelling the future by examining the liver of a sacrificed animal. The procedure was as follows: a sacrificial animal (commonly a sheep) was chosen; the priest slaughtered it according to established ritual; the liver was extracted and placed upon a specially prepared clay model; the priest then examined with the utmost care the shape, colour, and markings of the liver, comparing them with a standard "normal liver" model; and from the differences thus observed, he pronounced his prophecy of good or ill fortune.

Yale University possesses no fewer than thirty-two Babylonian clay liver models (circa 1900-1600 BCE), each divided into some fifty regions, every region bearing its own name and significance.

## Chapter II. Of the Egyptian System of Medicine, and the Origin of Reflex Therapy

> **Central Proposition**: The reflex therapy and herbal medicine of Ancient Egypt constitute one of the true sources of modern Reflexology; the papyri of Egypt contain the earliest systematic records of "part-whole" medical observation.

### § 2.1. Of the Rise of Egyptian Medicine

About the year 3100 before our era, Pharaoh Menes united Upper and Lower Egypt, establishing the first centralised imperial state known to history. For the succeeding three thousand years — from the Old Kingdom to the Ptolemaic dynasty — the civilisation of Egypt pursued its uninterrupted course, and its medical traditions underwent a complete evolution.

The Egyptians entertained a profound veneration for water, derived from the annual inundation of the Nile, whose fertilising waters transformed the desert into a garden — whence arose the conception of **water as the principle of life itself**. This metaphor was extended to the human body, wherein the "flood-ebb-flood" cycle of the Nile was likened to the ebb and flow of the bodily humours — the earliest foreshadowing of the Hippocratic doctrine of the Four Humours.

### § 2.2. Of the Edwin Smith Papyrus (circa 1600 BCE)

The Edwin Smith Papyrus, purchased at Luxor in the year 1862 by the American Egyptologist whose name it bears, is the most ancient surgical treatise known to mankind. Although inscribed about the year 1600 before our era (during the Sixteenth or Seventeenth Dynasty), its contents are believed to be copies of texts originating in the Old Kingdom, perhaps a millennium earlier.

The papyrus, measuring four metres and sixty-eight centimetres in length by thirty-three centimetres in breadth, contains forty-eight clinical cases, arranged in strict order from the head to the foot — all being cases of external trauma.

**Of Case the First: A Wound of the Head.** The most celebrated of all the cases is that of a man with a wound penetrating to the bone of the skull:

> "If thou examinest a man with a wound in his head, penetrating to the bone, the skull being fractured and the membrane exposed: thou shouldst touch the wound with thy finger. If thou findest the fragments of the skull pricking under thy finger (like the sensation of passing the finger over copper) — then thou shouldst say: 'This is a case which I will not treat.'"

We observe here the earliest recorded instance of **palpatory diagnosis** — the physician employing his finger to assess the condition of the cranial fragments. This is precisely the method which Zhang Yingqing was to systematise, five thousand years later, as "holographic palpatory diagnosis."

**Of Case the Sixth: An Injury of the Spine.** The papyrus records a case wherein a man with an injury to the spine experienced numbness in one foot but not the other. The physician is instructed to touch the spine and observe whether the patient can move his legs and feet. If the patient feels not the physician's touch, the case is incurable.

Here we have a clear recognition, five thousand years since, of the **spinal injury producing peripheral numbness** — a phenomenon entirely consistent with Zhang Yingqing's doctrine of **neural segmental projection**.

The Edwin Smith Papyrus reveals to us three fundamental principles: that **observation**, not incantation, is the foundation of medicine; that **palpation**, not divination, is the key to diagnosis; and that **prognosis** is the physician's responsibility, not the gods'.

### § 2.3. Of the Ebers Papyrus (circa 1550 BCE)

The Ebers Papyrus, purchased at Thebes in the year 1872 by the German Egyptologist Georg Ebers, is the longest medical papyrus known to scholarship — extending to no less than twenty metres and twenty-three centimetres in length. It contains eight hundred and seventy-seven paragraphs, comprising more than seven hundred herbal prescriptions and forty-eight clinical cases.

The pharmaceutical catalogue of the Ebers Papyrus reveals that the ancient Egyptians employed **more than seven hundred medicinal substances**: vegetable drugs (aloes, myrrh, saffron), mineral drugs (salt, alum, lead, copper), animal drugs (honey, bile, egg-white, fat), and fermented preparations (beer, wine). Many of these substances remain in use to the present day — aloes for burns, honey for the healing of wounds.

Despite its greater rational content compared with the Edwin Smith Papyrus, the Ebers Papyrus still abounds in incantations:

> "O demon, thou who comest down from heaven, thou who springest from the earth, thou who risest from the water — thou shalt not enter this body, for he is the son of Ra, the Sun-god himself!"

The Kahun Papyrus, discovered in the year 1889 by the British archaeologist Flinders Petrie at the site of Kahun in the Faiyum Oasis, is the earliest known treatise on gynaecology. It contains seventeen gynaecological and three obstetrical cases, covering disorders of menstruation, infertility, pregnancy diagnosis, contraception, and difficult labour.

The papyrus describes a celebrated method of pregnancy diagnosis:

> "Let the woman sprinkle her urine upon the seeds of wheat and barley. If the wheat sprout, she shall bear a female child; if the barley sprout, a male child; if neither sprout, she shall not bear."

## Chapter III. Of the Medicine of Greece and Rome, and the Rationalisation of the Holographic Principle

> **Central Proposition**: The medicine of Ancient Greece and Rome constitutes the "rational turning-point" of Western medicine — Hippocrates brought the art of healing from the temple into the clinic, and Galen systematised the science of anatomy. ### § 3.1. Of the Asclepieia and the Birth of Greek Medicine

Before the age of Hippocrates, the practice of medicine in Greece was intimately connected with the worship of the healing god Asclepius. Throughout the Greek world, from the sixth century before our era, there arose sacred precincts known as **Asclepieia**, where the sick might seek the aid of the god. The three most celebrated were those of Epidaurus (in the Peloponnese, the largest and most renowned), of Cos (in the Aegean Sea, the birthplace of Hippocrates), and of Pergamon (in Asia Minor, where Galen was later to practise).

The method of treatment in these sanctuaries comprised five stages: purificatory bathing (katharsis), which cleansed the body; sacrificial offering (thysia), which purified the soul; dream incubation (enkoimesis), wherein the patient slept within the temple in the hope that the god would reveal a cure in a dream; prescribed treatment (therapeia), wherein the priests administered herbal remedies; and the regulation of exercise and diet, by which the whole constitution was restored to harmony.

From the holographic perspective, this fivefold regimen embodies the principle that the **body (part) and the soul (whole) must be treated together** — a principle which lies at the heart of Zhang Yingqing's holographic doctrine.

### § 3.2. Of Hippocrates and the Doctrine of the Four Humours

**Hippocrates** (circa 460-370 BCE) was born upon the island of Cos and is justly styled the Father of Western Medicine. The collection of writings that bears his name — the **Corpus Hippocraticum** — comprises some sixty treatises, probably the work of several generations of the Coan school.

The central doctrine of the Hippocratic school was the **Theory of the Four Humours**. According to this theory, the human body contains four principal fluids: **blood** (sanguis), which proceeds from the liver and partakes of the element of air; **yellow bile** (cholē), which proceeds from the spleen and partakes of the element of fire; **black bile** (melancholē), which proceeds from the stomach and partakes of the element of earth; and **phlegm** (phlegma), which proceeds from the brain and lungs and partakes of the element of water. Health consists in the proper balance of these four humours; disease arises from their imbalance.

Each humour is associated with a particular temperament: the sanguine, hot and moist; the choleric, hot and dry; the melancholic, cold and dry; and the phlegmatic, cold and moist. These temperaments correspond, in turn, to the four seasons of the year.

From the holographic perspective, the Theory of the Four Humours embodies the principle of **part-whole correspondence** in its earliest systematic form. Each humour (a part) reflects the constitution of the whole individual; each humour corresponds to a set of natural elements, seasons, and organs — the microcosm reflecting the macrocosm. When Zhang Yingqing propounded his holographic theory twenty-five centuries later, he was, in essence, re-systematising the same insight that Hippocrates had first articulated.

### § 3.3. Of the Clinical Method of Hippocrates

The Hippocratic school brought the art of clinical observation to a level not surpassed until modern times. Their method comprised four principal modes of examination, which bear a striking resemblance to the four methods of diagnosis in Chinese medicine.

**Inspection** (opsis) — the observation of the skin, the countenance, the posture, the respiration, and the excretions. **Palpation** (hapsis) — the examination of the abdomen for tumours, the liver and spleen for enlargement, and the pulse for its character. **Interrogation** (erōtēsis) — the taking of the patient's history, his family history, his dwelling-place, and his habits of diet. **Auscultation** (akroasis) — the listening to the sounds of the body.

These four methods correspond, with remarkable exactness, to the four diagnostic methods of Chinese medicine: **wang** (inspection), **wen** (auscultation and olfaction), **wen** (interrogation), and **qie** (palpation). The two systems arose independently, yet they arrived at almost identical diagnostic frameworks — a circumstance which strongly suggests that there exists a **universal structure of medical cognition**, grounded in the holographic principle.

### § 3.4. Of Galen and the Systematisation of Anatomy

**Claudius Galenus** (129-216 CE), whom we commonly call Galen, was the greatest physician of the Roman Empire. Born at Pergamon in Asia Minor, he studied at Alexandria, then the centre of medical learning, and from the year 162 practised in Rome, where he became physician to the Emperor Marcus Aurelius.

Galen's anatomical researches, conducted principally upon animals (for the dissection of human bodies was forbidden), were the most extensive of antiquity. He distinguished between long, flat, short, and irregular bones; described more than three hundred muscles; and made the crucial distinction between **motor nerves** and **sensory nerves**. He recognised that each nerve (a part) innervates a particular region of the body (the whole) — a concept which we may regard as the ancient precursor of Zhang Yingqing's **neural segmental projection**.

Galen's physiological system was founded upon the doctrine of the **three pneumata**, or vital spirits. The **natural pneuma** was elaborated in the liver and distributed through the veins to nourish the body; the **vital pneuma** was elaborated in the heart and distributed through the arteries to animate the body; and the **animal pneuma** was elaborated in the brain and distributed through the nerves to govern sensation and motion. This system, though erroneous in its details, correctly recognised that the body is a **complex of coordinated parts working together as a whole** — the same insight that underlies the holographic doctrine.

### § 3.5. Of Dioscorides and the Standardisation of Materia Medica

**Pedanius Dioscorides** (circa 40-90 CE), a military surgeon in the service of the Emperor Nero, composed the most complete herbal encyclopedia of antiquity — the **De Materia Medica**, in five volumes, describing more than six hundred medicinal substances. Each drug is described according to a standardised format: its appearance, its method of collection, its preparation, its therapeutic uses with their proper dosages, and its side effects and contraindications.

This standardised description is the prototype of the modern pharmacopoeia, preceding the Chinese *Bencao Gangmu* by more than fifteen centuries. ## Chapter IV. Of the Golden Age of Arabic Medicine, and the Preservation of the Holographic Tradition

### § 4.1. Of the Rise of Arabic Civilisation and the Translation Movement

In the year 622 of our era, the Prophet Muhammad removed from Mecca to Medina, marking the beginning of the Islamic era. Within two centuries, the Arab Empire had extended from the Arabian Peninsula across North Africa, the Iberian Peninsula, Central Asia, and Northern India — becoming one of the largest empires known to history.

Under the Abbasid Caliphate (750-850 CE), there was inaugurated the greatest project of scholarly translation that the world had yet seen — the **Graeco-Arabic Translation Movement**. In the year 830, the Caliph Al-Ma'mun established the **Bayt al-Hikma** (the House of Wisdom) at Baghdad, where more than one hundred medical, philosophical, mathematical, and astronomical works were translated from Greek into Arabic.

The House of Wisdom was the first **cross-cultural medical knowledge graph** in human history. It gathered together the medical traditions of Greece (the Hippocratic corpus), Rome (the works of Galen), India (the Charaka Samhita), Persia (the Sassanid medical literature), and China (certain texts that had reached the Arabs through the Silk Road). ### § 4.2. Of Rhazes and His Clinical Observations

**Abu Bakr al-Razi** (865-925 CE), known to the Latins as Rhazes and justly styled the Father of Arabic Medicine, was born at Rayy in Persia. He was in his youth an alchemist, a musician, and a philosopher before turning, in his thirtieth year, to medicine. He became director of the Hospital of Baghdad and composed the **Kitab al-Hawi** (The Comprehensive Book of Medicine), in twenty-three volumes.

Rhazes recorded numerous instances of the **part-whole principle** in medicine. He observed that the size and reactivity of the pupil reflect the state of the whole body; that the colour and odour of the urine reveal the condition of the internal organs; that the pulse varies with different diseases; and that the countenance displays the state of the whole constitution.

But Rhazes's most celebrated contribution was his treatise **On Smallpox and Measles**, wherein he first clearly distinguished between these two diseases. He described the clinical features of smallpox, proposed the concept of contagion, and recommended the isolation of patients as a preventive measure — recognising that the **individual patient (a part) affects the whole community (the whole)**.

### § 4.3. Of Avicenna and the Canon of Medicine

**Ibn Sina** (980-1037 CE), known to the Latins as Avicenna and styled the Prince of Physicians, was born at Bukhara in Persia (modern Uzbekistan). At the age of ten he had committed the Qur'an and the whole of Arabic literature to memory; at sixteen he was already practising medicine; at eighteen he became court physician after curing the Prince of Bukhara; and at twenty-one he completed his great work, the **al-Qanun fi al-Tibb** (The Canon of Medicine).

The Canon of Medicine is the greatest medical encyclopedia ever composed by a single author. It comprises five volumes: the first treats of general principles, anatomy, and the classification of diseases; the second describes more than seven hundred medicinal herbs; the third treats of particular diseases according to the organ system; the fourth treats of general diseases (fevers, tumours, wounds); and the fifth contains the pharmacopoeia of compound remedies.

The Canon served as the standard textbook of medicine in the universities of Europe for six centuries — until the seventeenth century — and was translated into Latin, profoundly influencing the medicine of the Renaissance.

Avicenna systematised the art of **pulse diagnosis** to a degree not previously attained. He distinguished **forty-eight varieties of pulse**, classified according to their length, strength, speed, rhythm, and shape — a more detailed system than the twenty-eight pulses of Chinese medicine. He likewise developed a complete system of **urine diagnosis**, based upon the colour, transparency, sediment, and odour of the urine.

Avicenna's pulse diagnosis and urine diagnosis together constitute a complete system of **"part-whole" diagnostic medicine** — the Arabic precursor of Zhang Yingqing's eight micro-systems.

### § 4.5. Of the Translation into Europe and the Birth of Renaissance Medicine

In the year 1126, Archbishop Raymond of Toledo established a school of translation in that city, where the medical works of the Arabs were rendered from Arabic into Latin. The most celebrated of the translators was Gerard of Cremona (1114-1187), who translated Avicenna's Canon of Medicine. Through these translations, Arabic medicine entered the universities of medieval Europe.

It taught the works of Hippocrates, Galen, and Avicenna side by side, and conferred the degree of Doctor of Medicine. It was from Montpellier and similar institutions that the medicine of the Renaissance was born — Vesalius's anatomy (1543), Harvey's circulation of the blood (1628), and Paracelsus's chemical medicine (1530).

## Chapter V. Of the Medical System of Ancient India, and the Ayurvedic Tradition of the Holographic Embryo

> **Central Proposition**: Ayurveda is the oldest holographic tradition in human history, ranking with Chinese medicine in antiquity. ### § 5.1. Of the Origins of Indian Medicine

The civilisation of the Indus Valley (Harappa and Mohenjo-daro) flourished from about 2500 to 1900 BCE, exhibiting a level of urban planning (with its streets, drains, and public baths) that was not surpassed until the age of Rome. There are indications of early medical practice — the use of herbs, the reverence for medicinal plants.

About the year 1500 BCE, the Aryan peoples entered India from the north-west, bringing with them the Vedic religion and its sacred texts. The **Rig Veda**, the most ancient of the Vedas, consists of hymns to the gods. The **Atharva Veda** (the Veda of Magic and Medicine) is the earliest Indian medical text, composed between 1500 and 1000 BCE. It contains more than one hundred herbal prescriptions, together with incantations, rituals, and surgical treatments.

The Atharva Veda presents the same dual paradigm that we have observed in Sumerian medicine — **herb plus incantation**. Disease is attributed to three causes: the punishment of the gods, the invasion of demons, and the natural imbalance of the bodily humours.

### § 5.2. Of Charaka and the Doctrine of the Three Doshas

**Charaka** (circa 300 BCE), the Father of Indian Medicine, composed the **Charaka Samhita**, the foundational text of Ayurveda. The work comprises eight volumes, one hundred and twenty chapters, and approximately eight thousand verses.

The central doctrine of Ayurveda is the **Tridosha** — the doctrine of the Three Humours. **Vata** (Wind) partakes of the elements of space and air; it is dry, light, and cold, and is seated in the large intestine, the nerves, and the bones. **Pitta** (Bile) partakes of the elements of fire and water; it is hot, sharp, and moist, and is seated in the small intestine, the liver, and the blood. **Kapha** (Phlegm) partakes of the elements of water and earth; it is heavy, moist, and cool, and is seated in the stomach, the lungs, and the lymph.

Health consists in the equilibrium of the three Doshas; disease arises from their imbalance. This is the same fundamental insight as the Hippocratic doctrine of the Four Humours — expressed, in the Indian version, with three elements rather than four.

Charaka developed a complete system of diagnosis, comprising **inspection** (observation of the body, the skin, the eyes, the tongue), **palpation** (including pulse diagnosis, the Nadi Pariksha), **interrogation** (the history and habits of the patient), and **olfaction** (the smelling of the urine, the faeces, the sweat, and the breath) — a fourfold method that corresponds, once again, to the four methods of Chinese diagnosis.

The pulse diagnosis of Charaka distinguishes between the superficial pulse (indicating Vata imbalance), the middle pulse (indicating Pitta imbalance), and the deep pulse (indicating Kapha imbalance) — a system that resonates with the Chinese differentiation of the pulse at the Cun, Guan, and Chi positions.

### § 5.3. Of Sushruta and the 107 Marma Points

**Sushruta** (circa 600 BCE), the Father of Indian Surgery, composed the **Sushruta Samhita**, the foundational text of Ayurvedic surgery. The work comprises six volumes, one hundred and eighty-six chapters, and approximately ten thousand verses.

Sushruta's surgical achievements are among the most remarkable in the history of medicine. He described more than three hundred surgical procedures, including **rhinoplasty** (the reconstruction of the nose using a forehead flap — a technique still in use today), **couching for cataract** (the removal of the opaque lens with a needle), **Caesarean section**, **lithotomy** (the removal of bladder stones), and **haemorrhoidectomy**. He described more than twenty varieties of surgical knife.

But the most significant of Sushruta's contributions to the holographic tradition is his doctrine of the **107 Marma points**. The word *marman* signifies, in Sanskrit, a "vital point" or "secret point." The Marma points are the 107 places upon the body where the physical part meets the spiritual whole. ### § 5.4. Of the Yogic System of the Seven Chakras

The body is further held to contain **seventy-two thousand Nadis** (energy channels), of which the three principal ones are Ida (the left channel, lunar and yin), Pingala (the right channel, solar and yang), and Sushumna (the central channel, balancing both). ### § 6.1. Of the Mythical Origins of Chinese Medicine

The origins of Chinese medicine are veiled in the mists of legend. From the oracular bone inscriptions of the Shang dynasty (circa 1500-1000 BCE), we possess the earliest written evidence of Chinese medicine. The doctrine of the **Meridians** (the Twelve Principal Meridians and the Eight Extraordinary Vessels) describes the channels through which the Qi flows.
